# Brave Hearts Publishing — Brand Soul & Emotional Alignment Audit

Audit date: June 30, 2026  
Environment: live WordPress staging site  
Scope: Home, About, Books, Teachers, Blog, Learning Hub, Contact, Shop, and all six product pages  
Method: read-only review of rendered public pages; no code or WordPress content was changed

## Executive summary

Brave Hearts Publishing now feels like an adventure education brand—not merely a small publisher with nature-themed books. The clearest language is specific, ownable, and emotionally alive:

- “Big Places. Brave Hearts.”
- “Real Learning. Real Wonder.”
- “Real place. Real science. Real choice.”
- “Stop. Breathe. Think. Choose.”
- “I can do hard things.”

Those lines create a coherent promise: a child enters through adventure, encounters something true, and leaves with greater courage. The product descriptions express this promise most completely. Home, About, Books, and Teachers have also moved decisively toward it.

The central weakness is the gap between philosophy and experience. Several pages state a meaningful idea and then hand the visitor to a generic WordPress or WooCommerce pattern. The Blog is called “Latest Posts.” Six Learning Hub cards all lead to the same Blog archive. Shop is an unframed product grid. Contact becomes procedural and cautious. The About page explains the mission but does not yet tell the human story that made the mission necessary.

Parents are given strong reasons to feel excitement, but trust needs more proof: named educator evidence, clearer resource deliverables, reading-level transparency, a branded contact identity, and consistent purchase availability. Teachers can see the educational possibilities, but cannot yet inspect a concrete resource package before committing. Children are offered extraordinary destinations, yet the site rarely gives them a small moment of direct discovery—a surprising fact, a question, a map, a field note, or an invitation to look more closely at the world outside the book.

### Overall alignment

| Dimension | Assessment | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| Adventure | Strong | Real destinations and energetic language are distinctive throughout the core pages. |
| Education | Strong in promise; moderate in proof | Science, geography, vocabulary, conservation, and reading support are named often, but samples and concrete resource details are limited. |
| Character | Strongest on product pages | Courage becomes specific through choice and action; elsewhere it is often a label rather than a lived idea. |
| Parent trust | Moderate–strong | Kirkus, age range, Lexile language, formats, and practical use cases help; reviews, policies, fulfilment clarity, and branded contact details need strengthening. |
| Teacher trust | Moderate | The intended uses are credible, but visible resource samples, standards, preparation time, and educator proof are missing or underdeveloped. |
| Child wonder | Moderate–strong | Destinations and covers invite wonder; interactive discovery and child-facing microcopy are limited. |
| Brand distinctiveness | Strong on authored pages; weak on system pages | Home, About, Books, Teachers, and products carry a soul. Blog, Shop, archives, and utility moments feel more generic. |

## Page-by-page findings

## Home

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

Distinctly an adventure education brand. “Big Places. Brave Hearts.,” “Real Learning. Real Wonder.,” and destination-led storytelling immediately separate the Homepage from a generic author or bookstore site. The real-world destinations, learning pillars, teacher pathway, and Learning Hub reinforce that distinction.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

- Adventure: very clear through the Mariana Trench, Mount Everest, and Amazon Rainforest.
- Education: clear through real science, geography, wildlife, conservation, and classroom language.
- Character: present through courage and kindness, but quieter than the other two pillars. “Real Courage” is a label; the page needs one short example of what courage means in the stories.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

Excitement is strong. Trust is helped by the ages 6–9 focus, Kirkus reference, parent/educator testimonials, and clear product links. Trust would improve with one concise “What your reader gets” proof block: reading level, chapter length, illustration support, educational topics, and fulfilment source.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The teacher pathway is visible and relevant. “Classroom-ready and no-prep” is a valuable promise, but it needs proof beside it: a resource preview, page count, subjects, preparation time, and what is actually included.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

The destinations are inherently compelling, and “the deepest,” “the highest,” and “the world’s largest rainforest” are strong hooks. The section would feel more child-centered with one irresistible question beneath each place—for example: “What glows where sunlight has never reached?”

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- “Books Built for the Reading Gap” is useful parent language but arrives very early and temporarily shifts the page from wonder to market positioning.
- The Learning Hub cards promise six different worlds but all lead to the same general Blog archive.
- The number of CTAs dilutes the emotional sequence. Several variations of “explore,” “choose,” and “shop” compete rather than build momentum.
- “Books First” is less inspiring than the philosophy surrounding it and makes the brand feel product-first rather than purpose-first.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Place a short philosophy section directly after the hero, before the product grid.
- Change “Books First. Adventure Second. Education Always.” to a line that matches the actual brand hierarchy: **“Adventure opens the door. Truth deepens the wonder. Character carries it home.”**
- Give each destination one child-facing question and one true fact.
- Route each Learning Hub card to a curated topic destination, not the same Blog page.
- Reduce the principal Homepage journey to three escalating actions: **Choose a real-world adventure**, **Keep exploring**, **Bring it into the classroom**.
- Add a small visual motif such as field notes, map coordinates, altitude/depth markers, or a “Look closer” fact card. It should feel editorial, not game-like.

### 8. Keep unchanged

- “Big Places. Brave Hearts.”
- “Real Learning. Real Wonder.”
- The real-place destination structure.
- The six “what makes the series different” ideas.
- The age focus and teacher pathway.
- The warm, confident visual identity and book-cover prominence.

## About

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

Clearly an adventure education brand. The page names real places, real science, curiosity, courage, and care for the natural world. It explains what the publisher believes, not only what it sells.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

All three are present. Education and adventure are explicit; character appears through courage, confidence, and care. The principles are clear, but they are presented as a polished framework rather than a human conviction earned through a particular moment.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

The mission and ages 6–9 focus build trust. Andrew’s presence helps, but the founder copy remains biographical and declarative. Parents will trust the mission more when they understand why he could not leave the first story unwritten.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The page names read-alouds, discussion, vocabulary, STEM connections, homeschool use, and unit studies. That is useful, though educator trust would be stronger with a short link to evidence or sample resources rather than another general CTA.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

Children are described as curious, but the page speaks almost entirely to adults. A photograph or illustration of Andrew, Charlotte, and Henry connected to a real landscape would make the origin tangible and memorable.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- “Meet the Founder” is accurate but conventional.
- “Andrew created Brave Hearts Publishing to…” summarizes the outcome without showing the spark.
- The page lacks a vulnerable, sensory origin moment.
- “Books First” again understates the deeper purpose.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Add an origin section built around the first story, Charlotte, Henry, and the moment Andrew realized the real world could compete for a child’s attention.
- Include one concrete sensory detail: the hammock in Baja California, the landscape, the question that began the story, or the real child and dog behind the names. Confirm every biographical detail before publication.
- Replace the concluding hierarchy with the three-part philosophy line recommended for Home.
- Add a portrait or candid real-world image rather than another abstract brand graphic.
- CTA: **“Meet Charlotte, Henry, and the first adventure.”**

### 8. Keep unchanged

- “Build Curiosity Through Adventure.”
- “Stories That Make Children Want to Know More.”
- The four pillars: real places, real science, curiosity and courage, care for the natural world.
- The clear audience definition and practical reading contexts.
- Andrew’s direct identification as founder and author.

## Books

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

Strongly branded. The page leads with a growing series and a world of discovery, then organizes the books by real destination rather than format alone.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

Adventure and education are immediate. Character appears in courage and kindness but is not equally developed on the page. Each destination card would benefit from one character choice alongside its science/geography promise.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

The age range, short chapters, illustrations, format choices, and reading-gap positioning are practical and reassuring. “Start with Book 1” is a strong decision-reducing CTA. Excitement is high because each book has a clear geographic identity.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The classroom section names useful applications, but remains broad. Teachers need to know which book supports which standards, approximate read-aloud time, vocabulary load, and available downloads.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

The “deepest, highest, largest” progression is excellent. Add one short mystery or question to each card so the books feel like doors rather than products.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- “Kindle, Paperback, and Hardcover” is a retail heading, and the current cards visibly emphasize paperback/hardcover. It interrupts the story-led progression.
- “Choose the format that fits your reader” is useful but generic.
- Repeated “View Book” and “Shop Formats” CTAs are clear but emotionally neutral.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Make each book card answer three questions: **Where will we go? What will we discover? What courage will we practice?**
- Use destination-specific CTAs: **“Dive into the Mariana Trench,” “Climb toward Everest,” “Enter the Amazon canopy.”** Keep an adjacent plain-language purchase link for accessibility and clarity.
- Add a small “For growing readers” strip with Lexile/grade, chapter structure, illustrations, and read-aloud suitability.
- Confirm available formats and remove Kindle from the heading unless a Kindle path is live.
- Add a series map that shows the three real destinations without redesigning the page.

### 8. Keep unchanged

- “Explore the Charlotte & Henry Adventures.”
- “Stories That Travel Somewhere Real.”
- “One Growing Series. A World of Discovery.”
- “Start with Book 1” and “Shop All Books.”
- The destination-first card structure.

## Teachers

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

Distinctly an adventure education resource page. It understands that story is the bridge into science, geography, vocabulary, conservation, writing, and social-emotional learning.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

All three are clear. The individual book themes—ocean science and kindness, mountain geography and resilience, ecosystems and conservation—make the philosophy concrete.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

Parents can see that the books continue beyond entertainment. The page could reassure families that resources are useful at home, too, without blurring its primary teacher purpose.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The categories of support are excellent, but the actual deliverable remains abstract. “Lesson plans,” “discussion guides,” “vocabulary,” “maps,” and “printables” need visible examples or metadata. The page currently asks teachers to trust the promise before seeing the resource.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

The real destinations and cross-curricular themes support wonder. A resource preview showing a map, a surprising fact, or a discussion question would reveal how that wonder reaches the classroom.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- Resource labels read like a feature list rather than a teacher’s transformed classroom moment.
- The page lacks named educator proof in the reviewed content.
- “Get Teacher Resource Updates” asks for an email without showing what the teacher receives now.
- Author visits are appealing, but cautious availability language slightly outweighs warmth.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Add a trust section with resource page counts, grade band, preparation time, subjects, learning outcomes, and one preview image per book.
- Add two or three verified educator quotes with name, role, school/organization, and permission.
- Make the primary CTA immediate and concrete: **“Preview the free Mariana Trench teacher guide.”** Secondary: **“Bring a read-aloud to your school.”**
- Show one sample sequence: **Read the chapter → Ask the brave question → Explore the true science → Reflect on the choice.**
- Rewrite the visit language around possibility before limitations.

### 8. Keep unchanged

- “Bring the Adventure Into Your Classroom.”
- “Teach Through Story.”
- The six resource types.
- The book-by-book science, geography, ELA, SEL, and character mapping.
- The school, library, homeschool, and family audience breadth.

## Blog

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

The article subjects are highly aligned, but the archive presentation is generic. “Latest Posts,” dates, email-address bylines, and chronological cards make a rich learning library feel like a standard WordPress blog.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

Education is strong. Adventure is present in science and destination articles. Character is inconsistent and depends on the individual post. The archive does not explain how its topics belong to one philosophy.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

The reading-roadmap, Lexile, reluctant-reader, and recommendation posts are genuinely useful. The visible author identity as an email address weakens polish and trust. Parents need curated starting points instead of a long chronology.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

Teacher-relevant material exists but is difficult to identify quickly. Add visible topic and audience labels such as **Parents**, **Teachers**, **Read-Alouds**, **Science**, and **Reading Growth**.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

The headlines often contain excellent wonder hooks, especially “For the Kid Who Always Asks Why.” The archive itself is adult-oriented and does not offer a child-safe “Explore with a grown-up” path.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- “Latest Posts.”
- Repeated date/byline blocks.
- “Read more →.”
- No opening editorial statement connecting the articles to the brand philosophy.
- Chronology takes precedence over discovery.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Rename the page **“Field Notes for Curious Readers”** or **“Explore the Real World.”**
- Add the introduction: **“Questions that begin in a story should not end on the final page. Follow them into science, reading, wild places, and the brave work of growing up.”**
- Replace the email-address byline with **Andrew Signore**.
- Add curated entry points: **Help a Growing Reader**, **Explore a Real Place**, **Bring It to the Classroom**, **Practice Courage and Curiosity**.
- Replace generic “Read more” with topic-led labels such as **“Follow the question →”** or **“Explore the field note →.”** Use consistently.

### 8. Keep unchanged

- The parent-help content around reading gaps and reluctant readers.
- The science and real-place editorial direction.
- The frank, empathetic headlines that understand parents’ actual problems.
- The Kirkus and origin-story articles as trust-building editorial anchors.

## Learning Hub

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

The concept is highly distinctive; the current behavior is not. Animals, Science, Geography, Conservation, Explorers, and Activities form a strong adventure-education taxonomy, but all six cards currently lead to the same Blog archive.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

Adventure and education are explicit. Character is implied in Conservation and Explorers but needs a clearer thread: how does learning about this subject shape the child who is learning?

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

The taxonomy creates excitement, then disappoints because the destinations are not specific. Parents may interpret the cards as six developed hubs when they are presently six labels for one archive.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The topics could become excellent classroom pathways. Without curated destinations, grade bands, resource types, or learning objectives, they do not yet function as dependable teacher resources.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

This should be the most child-curious part of the site. It currently offers category names rather than discoveries.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- Identical generic destination for all six cards.
- No true fact, question, featured field note, or activity preview.
- No visual distinction beyond category treatment.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Give each card a curated landing destination or filtered archive with a distinct introduction.
- Add a child-facing question:
  - Animals: **“How does anything live where the pressure can crush steel?”**
  - Science: **“Why does the ocean glow in complete darkness?”**
  - Geography: **“How can the highest and deepest places belong to the same planet?”**
  - Conservation: **“What changes when a child decides a wild place matters?”**
  - Explorers: **“What makes someone take the next step when the map runs out?”**
  - Activities: **“Can you test pressure, map a mountain, or notice a habitat at home?”**
- Add one featured item and item count per hub so the promise feels real.
- Use field-journal, map, specimen, and observation imagery rather than generic category icons.

### 8. Keep unchanged

- The six-topic structure.
- The phrase “The Learning Hub.”
- The connection between stories and continued discovery.
- The prominent Homepage placement.

## Contact

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

The opening—“Let’s Start the Next Adventure”—is on-brand. The rest becomes a conventional inquiry page with cautious operational language.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

Adventure is present in the framing. Education appears in school, library, and read-aloud pathways. Character is not especially relevant here, though warmth, generosity, and respect should be felt in the response promise.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

Clear inquiry paths help. Trust is weakened by a personal iCloud mailto address rather than a Brave Hearts domain address, and by the absence of a visible response expectation.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The page tells teachers what information to provide for a read-aloud request. It would be stronger with location/service-area guidance, typical program length, audience size, virtual/in-person possibilities, and response timing.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

This is primarily an adult utility page, appropriately. A small warm image of a read-aloud or school visit would make the invitation feel real without making the page childish.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- “Submitting an inquiry does not guarantee availability” is necessary but receives too much emotional weight.
- “Use the email option” and “Send a message” are procedural.
- No human sign-off or expectation of what happens next.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Lead with: **“Tell us where you hope the next adventure will begin.”**
- Add: **“You’ll hear from a real person at Brave Hearts Publishing within two business days.”** Use only if operationally true.
- Use a branded domain email address.
- Add a concise read-aloud information panel: format, audience, duration, location, and what students will experience.
- Move the availability disclaimer beneath the inquiry details in quieter language.
- Add a candid classroom/read-aloud visual with appropriate permission.

### 8. Keep unchanged

- “Let’s Start the Next Adventure.”
- The three inquiry pathways.
- The explicit warning not to include private student information.
- The direct links back to Books and Teachers.

## Product pages

### 1. Adventure education brand or generic book website?

These are the strongest expressions of the brand soul. Each page begins with an extraordinary true fact, enters a real place, identifies a character choice, and connects that choice to the child.

### 2. Adventure, education, and character

All three are integrated rather than listed. The science creates stakes; the stakes create a choice; the choice builds courage. This is the model the rest of the site should follow.

### 3. Parent trust and excitement

The descriptions create excitement and answer practical questions: age, grade, Lexile range, read-aloud use, classroom use, format, printing, and shipping. “Kids who say they don’t like to read” shows empathy. Trust is weakened by “Reviews (0),” inconsistent hardcover availability, and claims of a free teacher guide without an immediate visible guide CTA.

### 4. Teacher usefulness and credibility

The grade/Lexile/read-aloud details are helpful. Add a direct teacher-resource link and a small curriculum summary specific to each book.

### 5. Child curiosity and wonder

Excellent. “The ocean floor is seven miles below the surface,” “a candle won’t stay lit,” and “every fifth breath” are memorable true hooks. The phrasing respects children’s intelligence.

### 6. Emotionally flat areas

- Generic WooCommerce tabs: “Description” and “Related products.”
- The long product taxonomy/tag list interrupts the emotional narrative.
- “Add to Cart” is functional but isolated from the deeper promise.
- Related products are format-heavy rather than journey-led.

### 7. Specific improvements

- Add a three-part summary near the purchase control: **Discover**, **Practice**, **Keep Exploring**.
- Place **“Get the free teacher guide”** beside the classroom-use claim when a working resource exists.
- Rename the visible narrative section **“Inside the Adventure”** while retaining accessible WooCommerce structure.
- Reframe related products as **“Choose the next real-world adventure.”**
- Add a short reassurance near purchase: intended reader, illustrated format, printer/fulfilment, and shipping expectations.
- Keep functional “Add to Cart” text, but pair it with a nearby emotional line: **“Take the first dive,” “Begin the climb,”** or **“Enter the rainforest.”**

### 8. Keep unchanged

- The opening true facts for all three destinations.
- “Stop. Breathe. Think. Choose.”
- “I can do hard things.”
- “Every challenge is a decision point, not just a plot point.”
- “Charlotte is a real child. Henry is a real dog. [Destination] is a real place.”
- The practical reader/use-case details.
- The destination-specific character lesson, especially Everest’s recognition that turning back can be brave.

## Highest-impact emotional improvements

1. **Put the philosophy into one unforgettable Homepage section.** The ideas exist throughout the site, but visitors should encounter their clearest expression before the product catalog.
2. **Turn the Learning Hub from a promise into six real pathways.** This is the biggest gap between the brand’s educational ambition and the current experience.
3. **Tell the human origin story on About.** Principles become believable when visitors understand the person, child, dog, and place behind them.
4. **Make teacher trust visible.** Show the resource, quantify it, preview it, and let an educator validate it.
5. **Carry the product-page voice into system pages.** Shop and Blog need editorial framing; Contact needs human warmth.
6. **Give children something true to wonder about on every major page.** A question, observation, field note, or fact is enough.
7. **Use character as an action, not an adjective.** Replace generic courage claims with choices: breathe, ask, help, turn back, try again, care for what is wild.

## Copy recommendations

### Core brand articulation

**Primary philosophy:**  
Nature is the greatest classroom on Earth.

**Supporting promise:**  
We do not ask children to leave the real world behind. We invite them to look closer, travel farther, ask better questions, and fall in love with the world already waiting for them.

**Three-part line:**  
Adventure opens the door. Truth deepens the wonder. Character carries it home.

**Closing conviction:**  
The world has not changed. The child has. Our job is to help them see it again.

### CTA language system

Use emotional language to frame the moment and plain language to preserve clarity.

| Context | Recommended primary CTA | Recommended supporting CTA |
|---|---|---|
| Home | Choose a real-world adventure | Bring wonder to your classroom |
| About | Meet Charlotte, Henry, and the first adventure | Explore the books |
| Books | Start with the Mariana Trench | Compare editions |
| Teachers | Preview a free teacher guide | Request a read-aloud |
| Blog | Follow the question | Browse by topic |
| Learning Hub | Explore [topic] | Try an activity |
| Contact | Tell us where the adventure begins | Request a read-aloud |
| Product | Add to Cart | Get the teacher guide |

### Words to favor

Real place, true, discover, notice, explore, field note, question, choose, courage, wonder, classroom, wild, growing reader, look closer.

### Words to use sparingly

Content, resources without specifics, latest posts, learn more, read more, products, results, educational without evidence, adventure when it is not attached to an action or place.

**Heading:** The Real World Is Still Wild Enough

**Body:**  
Nature is the greatest classroom on Earth. Brave Hearts stories begin in places that are real—the deepest ocean, the highest mountain, the largest rainforest—and follow the questions those places awaken.

We do not ask children to escape the world. We invite them to look closer. To discover something true. To practice courage when the path changes. To come home seeing their own planet, and themselves, differently.

**Heading:** A Real Child. A Real Dog. A World Worth Noticing.

The first Charlotte & Henry story began with two real names and a simple belief: the world had not become less extraordinary. Children had simply been given fewer reasons to stop and look closely at it.

Andrew Signore began writing after his niece Charlotte was born. Henry was already there—the real dog who would follow her into every impossible place on the page. What started as words written for one child became a larger promise to growing readers everywhere.
